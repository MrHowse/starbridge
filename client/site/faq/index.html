Frequently Asked Questions

STARBRIDGE SUPPORT REFERENCE

How do I join a game?

Open your browser and navigate to the server address (ask the host for the URL, or use http://localhost:8000 if running locally). Click PLAY on the landing page to go to the lobby. Enter your callsign and press ENTER. You will see a list of station roles — click CLAIM next to the station you want to operate.

If a game is already in progress, you can still join a role that has not been claimed. The server will broadcast game.started to you so your station catches up with the current game state.

How many players do I need?

It depends on the ship class. The minimum crew requirement ranges from 3 (Scout) to 10 (Battleship). The recommended size for a full experience is 4–8 players on a Corvette or Frigate.

Smaller crews can still play — unclaimed stations still function, they just can't react to events on their own. The Captain or Engineering player often monitors multiple stations in small-crew games.

The lobby shows a soft minimum-crew warning if you try to launch below the ship's recommended crew count. You can override it with a second click.

Can I play solo?

Yes — all stations can be navigated in separate browser tabs or windows on the same machine. Claim multiple roles (you can add secondary roles from the role bar at the bottom of any station). It's hectic, but workable for learning the game or testing missions.

The training missions are specifically designed for solo play — each one focuses on a single station, and the server runs automated control for all other roles so you can practise in isolation.

How do I switch roles mid-game?

Every station has a role bar along the bottom of the screen showing all crew roles and who holds them. Click any open (unclaimed) slot to navigate to that station. You can also click your own role on a different station to navigate back to it — your role pill appears with a green highlight.

Switching navigates your browser to the new station's page. Your original station remains active on the server — another player can claim it, or you can return to it from the role bar.

Why can't I hear any sound?

Most browsers block audio until the user interacts with the page. Click anywhere on your station to unblock audio — you should hear the life support ambient hum start shortly after game.started.

If sound still doesn't play, check that your browser's site permissions allow audio (some browsers mute new tabs by default). Also confirm your system volume is not muted and the correct audio output device is selected.

If you prefer silence, the accessibility widget (⚙ bottom-right) does not control audio, but you can mute the tab directly in your browser.

My station isn't updating — what do I do?

First check the connection indicator in the top-left corner of your station. If it shows DISCONNECTED (red), the WebSocket connection to the server has been lost. The client attempts to reconnect automatically every few seconds — wait 10–15 seconds and watch for the indicator to turn green again.

If reconnection fails, hard-refresh the page (Ctrl+Shift+R / Cmd+Shift+R). Your role will still be reserved for a short period after disconnect, so you should reconnect cleanly.

If the indicator shows CONNECTED but data has stopped: the game may be paused by the admin. Check with the admin or Captain.

How do I save and resume a game?

Only an admin can save and resume — crew stations don't have save controls. Go to the admin dashboard, find the Save section, and click SAVE GAME. This creates a timestamped snapshot in the saves/ directory.

To resume later, open the admin dashboard, find the save in the list, and click RESUME. The server restores the world state and broadcasts game.started to any connected clients. Crew can then navigate to their stations as normal.

How do I change the difficulty?

Difficulty is set in the lobby before launching (the host selects a preset), and can also be changed mid-game from the admin dashboard. Available presets:

  • Cadet: Reduced enemy damage, faster repairs, more generous resource pools. Recommended for first sessions.
  • Officer: Default balanced settings.
  • Commander: Increased enemy aggression, slower healing, tighter time limits on objectives.
  • Admiral: Maximum difficulty — enemy damage is severe, resources are scarce, and mistakes are rarely recoverable.

Changing difficulty mid-game takes effect immediately on the next tick.

How do I create my own missions?

Use the visual mission editor at /editor/. The editor lets you build mission graphs — nodes (objectives, branches, parallel tasks, checkpoints) connected by edges (sequence, branch triggers, actions). No JSON knowledge required.

Missions are saved to the server's missions/ directory and appear in the lobby mission list immediately. You can also edit mission JSON files directly if you prefer — see the graph format documentation in the editor's built-in help.

The editor includes a built-in validator that checks for unreachable nodes, missing victory conditions, and duplicate puzzle labels before you save.

What do the alert levels mean?

Alert level is set by the Captain and changes the visual and audio tone across all stations simultaneously:

  • GREEN alert: Routine operations. Normal ambient sound. Standard green wire aesthetic.
  • YELLOW alert: Caution — enemy detected or situation is developing. Amber primary colour, elevated ambient sound.
  • RED alert: Immediate danger — active combat or critical system failure. Red primary colour, combat-level ambient sound.

Alert level does not directly affect game mechanics (enemies aren't harder at red alert). It is a communication and situational-awareness tool for the crew.

How does the power budget work?

Nine ship systems share a fixed budget of 900 power units (100 per system at default). Engineering can redistribute power freely — raise one system by 50 and the other 50 must come from somewhere else. The total cannot exceed 900.

A system's efficiency scales with its power allocation. At 100 power, a system runs at 100% efficiency (modified by health and crew factor). At 50 power, roughly 50% efficiency. This affects:

  • Engines → ship speed and turn rate
  • Shields → shield capacity and regen rate
  • Sensors → detection range and scan quality
  • Weapons → beam damage and fire rate
  • Life Support → crew health recovery rate
  • Flight Deck → drone speed and probe range
  • ECM Suite → jam range and effectiveness
  • Point Defence → torpedo intercept probability

Systems damaged below 50 HP also suffer an efficiency penalty proportional to their health — full power to a broken system still underperforms.

What ship class should I pick?

For new groups: start with the Corvette (Sentinel-class, hull 90, crew 4–6). It has enough hull to survive early mistakes without being overwhelming to manage.

For experienced crews of 6–9: the Frigate (Resolution-class, hull 120) is the default and offers a balanced experience across all mission types.

If your group loves Flight Operations specifically: the Carrier (Atlas-class, hull 200) expands the drone bay and probe stock significantly.

If you want maximum challenge: the Battleship (Sovereign-class, hull 300, crew 10–12) requires every station staffed and active coordination to manage effectively.

How do I dock at a space station?

Helm reduces throttle to 10% or below and flies within the station's docking range (shown on the Science sensor map). Once in range, the docking clearance request is sent automatically. Friendly stations grant clearance after ~2 seconds; neutral stations take ~4 seconds. The 10-second docking sequence then begins — hold course until complete.

While docked, the ship is stationary and shields cap at 50%. Services start automatically and run in parallel. Helm can undock at any time (5-second sequence), or the Captain can trigger an emergency undock instantly.

Some missions require docking with a specific station as an objective — check the station's type first (repair dock, research, derelict). Derelict stations should be BIO-scanned by Science before approach to confirm no hull leeches.

How do I deal with space creatures?

Each creature type has a preferred interaction method — combat is rarely the best option:

  • Void whale: Docile. Science uses BIO scan mode to study it (60 seconds of proximity). Does not attack; flees if approached too closely.
  • Rift stalker: Territorial. EW can sedate it, Comms can negotiate, or you can fight it. Sedation takes 120 seconds to wear off. It regenerates 2 HP/s, so prolonged combat is costly.
  • Hull leech: Attaches to the hull and drains 3 HP every 5 s. Science BIO scan reveals its location. Remove via: Engineering electrical discharge, depressurise the room (Security seals doors), or EVA (Security). Any method works — do it fast.
  • Swarm: EW dispersal is most effective; Science first studies it to reveal the correct dispersal frequency. Weapons fire works but takes longer.
  • Leviathan: 800 HP, very dangerous. Attempt Communication (Comms) or Science study before engaging. Direct combat requires full crew coordination and high shield/hull reserves.
What do the four scan modes (EM / GRAV / BIO / SUB) do?

Scan mode determines which entity types appear first (and most clearly) during a sector sweep or long-range scan. The mode affects both entity scans and area scans:

  • EM (Electromagnetic): Prioritises energy sources — stations, powered outposts, transponders. Use in combat or when looking for stations.
  • GRAV (Gravitational): Prioritises mass concentrations — asteroid fields, gravity wells, derelicts, debris clouds. Use when planning routes or looking for salvage.
  • BIO (Biological): Prioritises life signs — creatures, inhabited stations, disease outbreaks. Essential before docking with a derelict or before approaching unknown creatures.
  • SUB (Subspace): Prioritises subspace phenomena — anomalies, signal relays, cloaked contacts, distortions. Use when triangulating a mission signal source.

For a sector sweep, choose the mode that matches your current objective. All entities are eventually revealed regardless of mode — mode only affects which ones appear first in the 30-second sweep cycle.

How does shield focus work?

Shields are split into four facings: Fore (front), Aft (rear), Port (left), and Starboard (right). The total shield capacity (200 HP) is distributed across all four facings based on a focus point.

On the Weapons station, drag the focus point on the shield canvas toward the threat direction (or use arrow keys to nudge, X/Y to lock axes, C to centre). Shield energy shifts to the nearest facing — for example, dragging forward concentrates shields on the fore arc. The Captain's display shows a four-bar readout of current shield distribution.

Use the LOCK button to freeze the focus point. Use CENTRE (or C key) to reset to an even 50/50/50/50 split. When incoming fire hits a specific facing, only that facing's shield pool absorbs the damage — if it runs out, hull takes the remainder.

How does the Medical station work?

Medical tracks individual crew members, not just deck totals. Each crew member can sustain injuries to specific body regions (head, torso, arms, legs) at four severity levels: minor, moderate, serious, and critical.

Injuries degrade over time if untreated — a moderate wound becomes serious, serious becomes critical, and critical injuries are fatal. Your job is triage: prioritise the most urgent cases.

Treatment types:

  • First Aid — fast, for minor/moderate injuries.
  • Stabilise — pauses degradation; buys time when beds are full.
  • Surgery — for serious injuries; triggers a triage puzzle.
  • Intensive Care — for critical injuries; harder puzzle, higher supply cost.
  • Quarantine — isolates contagious crew to prevent outbreaks.

The body diagram shows injury locations colour-coded by severity. Crew factor (the ratio of healthy crew to total crew on a deck) directly affects that deck's system efficiency — keeping crew healthy keeps the entire ship running.
